# Direction Experiments

Turn a tentative direction into a cheap encounter with reality. The purpose is not to declare an identity or pick a permanent career. It is to expose important uncertainty to observable evidence while the cost of being wrong is still low.

## Three possible selves

Create exactly three possible-self cards. Make them materially different: vary the role, the people served, and the mode of work rather than merely changing the topic or title. At least one may be a low-evidence new identity that seems worth a cheap rehearsal. Label that uncertainty plainly; novelty is permission to test, not evidence that the identity fits.

Use the canonical source-observation and current-AI-inference records in `evidence-mirror.md`; do not recreate or alter that schema here. In each card, cite source-observation IDs and source references, then show the linked AI interpretation separately. As a card-specific presentation rule, give the interpretations about ability; demand, relevance, or uptake; and energy a confidence of `strong`, `mixed`, or `weak`. If support is thin or conflicting, explain what is unknown rather than smoothing it into confidence.

Each card contains only:

- **Role + people + problem:** what role this self inhabits, which beneficiary, stakeholder, peer, practice community, or consequential work context it serves, and which concrete problem or practice it advances.
- **Sourced evidence:** concise source observations for ability; demand, relevance, or uptake; and energy, followed by clearly separate AI interpretation; mark confidence `strong`, `mixed`, or `weak` and include the strongest contradiction.
- **Biggest unknown:** the one uncertainty most likely to change the decision.
- **Observable Falsifier:** what result would lower confidence in the tested hypothesis component, not in the whole possible self.
- **Smallest identity rehearsal:** the cheapest real act that briefly requires behaving as this self.

Render one short bullet for each of these five fields by default. Keep the full evidence ledger available on request instead of crowding the cards.

Do not use a total score, weighted ranking, or winner. The cards reveal different uncertainties and evidence gaps; explain those differences without pretending unlike evidence is commensurable.

## Choose one experiment

Compare the cards by asking which role the user is willing to enter, not which description they admire. Favor the experiment that can produce the lowest-cost, fastest decisive new information—including bad news—about the biggest unknown.

The experiment must engage a real beneficiary, stakeholder, peer, practice community, or consequential work context; a real problem or practice; and the actual mode of work. This includes directions centered on research, craft, infrastructure, or art rather than direct service. Prefer a test that uses or develops an ability that is scarce, compounding, or both. Ask the user to select one possible self before expanding the experiment. Retain the other two in the conversation as later options without calling them rejected, inferior, or failed; persist them only when the user explicitly requests it.

## Safety and outreach gate

The user performs all outreach and delivery. The agent only drafts materials. In this workflow, the agent never contacts, sends, posts, publishes, recruits, or delivers; the user always performs those actions.

Before execution, ask the user to:

- make participation respectful and voluntary, state the experiment's purpose, and honor a decline or no response;
- minimize and de-identify collected data, and obtain consent before retaining quotes or personal details;
- make no repeated unsolicited follow-ups—at most one polite follow-up;
- keep the work small, bounded, reversible, and within their competence.

For medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, safeguarding, or vulnerable-person contexts, do not test a direction by providing unqualified advice or service. Use public-source study, informational interviews with qualified practitioners, or qualified supervision. Any observation involving a regulated setting or vulnerable people must either be public, or have both appropriate consent and institutional or professional approval; being non-sensitive alone is insufficient.

## Seven-day experiment

Expand only the selected possible self. Within seven days, plan two respectful outreach attempts to people doing the same or an adjacent practice. Completed conversations are stronger evidence but not a required outcome. When an outreach attempt would be unsafe, inappropriate, or impractical, one relevant public talk, published case study, or supervised observation substitutes for one outreach attempt. Record which attempt it replaced and why; two substitutions may replace both attempts when the reason applies to both.

Attempt to help one real target only when doing so is safe, consensual, and within the user's competence. A target may be a beneficiary, stakeholder, peer, or practice community. When direct help is inappropriate or the direction is research, craft, infrastructure, or art, perform a small piece of consequential work in a faithful context instead. Passive admiration is insufficient, but the experiment must not manufacture interpersonal risk merely to feel real.

Match the medium to the work being tested: content, interview, consulting, service, tool, workshop, or another faithful medium. Do not translate every direction into publishing.

Preregister the experiment before execution. Define:

- **Hypothesis component:** the specific demand, relevance, or uptake claim—or ability, energy, or role-fit claim—this week can genuinely test.
- **Biggest unknown:** the uncertainty whose resolution would change the next decision.
- **Action:** the smallest faithful piece of the role and mode of work.
- **Target/context:** the narrowly defined beneficiary, stakeholder, peer, practice community, or consequential work context and problem or practice.
- **Observation window:** the scheduled outreach, delivery, observation, and review dates within seven days.
- **Valid-test conditions:** target fit, planned exposure count and denominator, medium, and delivery fidelity required for interpretation.
- **Evidence:** the external behavior or outcome, faithful execution, internal experience, and skill or learning that will be recorded.
- **Disconfirming observation / Falsifier:** the observable result that would count against the tested component.
- **Update rule:** how each plausible outcome updates demand, relevance, or uptake; ability; energy; or role fit and leads to `continue`, `adjust`, or `stop`.

Freeze the preregistered interpretation rules during the run. Log deviations separately, including why they occurred and whether they invalidate the test; do not silently rewrite success criteria after seeing results.

Keep the test small enough to complete and real enough to disappoint. Record outreach attempts, responses, delivery, observed use, and exact observed behaviors rather than relying on recollection. “Exact commitments” means exact observed behavior, not verbatim personal detail.

## Signal and commitment record

Calibrate signals to the selected role and context; there is no universal ladder. Record every material signal, not only the highest one:

- observed signal behavior;
- cost in time, money, reputation, privacy, or effort;
- target fit;
- whether the source is a friend, unfamiliar target, peer, stakeholder, or other context;
- signal count;
- exposure denominator.

Payment is optional and role-specific. For example, in research the stronger signal may be expert scrutiny or reuse; in craft it may be sustained practice or commissioning; in infrastructure it may be adoption or maintainer time; in art it may be attentive engagement, invitation, collection, or repeat audience behavior.

A common commercial **Commitment ladder**—polite response or like < detailed question or story < submitted material or referral < booking, continued trial, or collaboration time < payment or repeat use—is only a non-universal example. Separate friends or close supporters from unfamiliar target users. Praise, encouragement, stated interest, and future promises are not commitment unless followed by relevant costly behavior.

## Internal learning

After performing the work, record:

- willingness to re-enter it after difficulty or awkwardness;
- experienced autonomy rather than fantasy autonomy;
- evidence of skill growth;
- visible progress in the person, problem, or artifact;
- a reframing, surprise, or changed understanding of the work.

Notice energy before, during, immediately after, and the next day. Energy loss can justify adjusting the people, problem, scope, cadence, or mode of work. Persistence is not a moral duty, and stopping a poorly fitting test is not a character verdict.

## Generate the minimum artifact

Create only the artifact required to run the selected experiment:

- **Content test:** a minimal recruiting post or first piece, with up to three candidate topics if topic choice is necessary.
- **Interview test:** a short, non-leading guide about past behavior, concrete episodes, present workarounds, and costs; do not ask respondents to predict whether they would use an imagined solution.
- **Service or consulting test:** a short offer, a recruitment message, and a first-delivery checklist.
- **Tool test:** a problem statement, intended use, and the smallest usable prototype.
- **Workshop test:** a recruitment message, minimum agenda, and behavior-focused feedback prompt.
- **Help-one-person test:** a bounded scope, observation notes, and an after-action review.

Do not default to content. The artifact must resemble the selected role's actual work closely enough to test the biggest unknown.

## Seven-day review

When the user returns with results from an experiment that had no preregistration, review cautiously. Record what actually happened and separate source observations from post-hoc interpretations. Do not retrofit a falsifier or pretend that target, exposure, fidelity, or update rules were fixed in advance. These observations may inform a tentative next hypothesis or practical decision, but they do not count as an update from a preregistered test and cannot be described as falsifying or confirming one. Treat conclusions as limited by the unknown conditions and never overclaim the evidence.

At the end of the week, ask:

1. **Executed:** What was actually attempted, delivered, and observed? Were the preregistered valid-test conditions met, and what deviations occurred?
2. **Signals:** What signal behaviors appeared, at what cost, from which target/context, in what count, and against what exposure denominator? Do not report only the highest signal.
3. **Contradiction:** What result most challenged the original evidence, hypothesis, or identity story?
4. **Internal change and learning:** How did willingness, autonomy, skill, progress, energy, or understanding change after faithful execution?
5. **Decision:** Integrate external response, execution fidelity, internal experience, and skill or learning to continue, adjust, or stop. State which observation supports the choice.

No feedback is missing data when preregistered valid exposure was not met. Diagnose reach, recruitment, target selection, message clarity, timing, medium, and delivery fidelity. When adequate preregistered exposure was met, silence is weak negative evidence only for the tested response, demand, relevance, or uptake component—not for ability, energy, role fit, or the user's whole identity.

One seven-day test updates only its preregistered hypothesis component. It never validates or invalidates a whole identity.

Only later, after the user shows enough evidence of reliable delivery and the direction offers skill growth or career capital, consider a 30–90 day project. Do not use a longer commitment to compensate for a seven-day experiment that never reached reality.
